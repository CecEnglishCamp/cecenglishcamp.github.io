지문 읽기
📡 텔레스크린이 당신을 감시합니다. B1으로 읽어드려요. C1은 수능 실전 수준이에요.

For years, Winston has watched O'Brien, a high-ranking Inner Party member. He believes, without any real evidence, that O'Brien secretly opposes the Party. Something in O'Brien's eyes seems to speak of hidden knowledge.

One day, O'Brien contacts Winston and invites him to his apartment. It is O'Brien who gives Winston a copy of "The Book" — supposedly written by the rebel leader Emmanuel Goldstein.

Winston reads the book eagerly. It explains how the Party maintains power not through wealth or luxury but through deliberate poverty and endless war. The book gives Winston's intuitions a theoretical framework.

Not until the very end does Winston discover that O'Brien is not an ally but the enemy — a senior Thought Police officer who has been watching Winston for years. The Brotherhood is a trap.

O'Brien is Orwell's most terrifying creation precisely because he is not a simple villain. He is intelligent, generous, and genuinely interesting — a man who understands Winston better than Winston understands himself. It is this understanding that makes his betrayal so complete: he does not simply arrest Winston; he dismantles him.

The Brotherhood — the underground resistance network — may or may not exist. Orwell never resolves this. What matters is that the Party can simulate it perfectly. Whether the resistance is real or fabricated, the outcome for Winston is identical: capture, torture, and re-education.

This is Orwell's most devastating argument: in a sufficiently total system, even hope becomes a tool of control. The dream of resistance is itself the mechanism of final defeat.

수능 유형 — 빈칸 추론
29번 유형
In 1984, Orwell suggests that the most effective form of totalitarian control is not brute force but the systematic elimination of hope. Winston's belief that O'Brien is a secret ally and that the Brotherhood exists gives him the will to resist — but this hope is itself engineered by the Party. The implication is that in a truly total system, By reducing the vocabulary available to citizens through Newspeak, the Party ensures that certain concepts become literally unthinkable. A person who lacks the words to express rebellion cannot rebel; a person who cannot conceive of an alternative to the present cannot imagine change. This is why Winston's act of writing in his diary is so significant: it is not merely an act of courage but an act of          — a refusal to allow his inner life to be colonised by the Party's language.
