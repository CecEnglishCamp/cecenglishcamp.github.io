지문 읽기
🔊 B1 기준으로 읽어드려요. C1은 수능 실전 수준이에요.

Jack discovers that he can paint his face with clay and charcoal. Having done so, he feels completely different — less like a choirboy, more like a hunter.

The paint hides his face. Behind the mask, Jack feels free from the shame and embarrassment he normally experiences. He is no longer the boy who failed to kill the pig. He is something else entirely.

Golding describes the mask as "a thing on its own." Behind it, Jack is liberated from self-consciousness. He dances and chants with his hunters. They kill their first pig.

What the paint allows Jack to do is shed his civilised identity. He does not just become a hunter — he becomes someone who enjoys the kill. This shift frightens Ralph and Piggy, who see in Jack's transformation a warning about what the island may do to all of them.

The face paint is Golding's most powerful symbol of identity dissolution. In civilised society, the face is the site of moral accountability — we are seen, judged, and held responsible through our faces. By covering his face, Jack removes himself from this system of mutual visibility and judgment.

Golding is drawing on a deep anthropological truth: anonymity enables violence. The mask does not create Jack's savagery; it removes the inhibitions that civilisation had placed over it. What is terrifying is not that Jack changes, but that the paint simply reveals what was always there.

The chanting and dancing that follow the first kill are not merely celebration — they are ritual. Ritual is the grammar of belonging; it transforms individuals into a collective, and in doing so, transfers moral responsibility from the person to the group.

잭은 점토와 목탄으로 얼굴을 칠할 수 있다는 것을 발견해요. 그렇게 하고 나서 그는 완전히 다르게 느껴요 — 합창단원이 아닌 사냥꾼처럼요. 가면 뒤에서 잭은 수치심에서 자유로움을 느껴요.

When Jack paints his face for the first time, Golding describes the result as "a thing on its own, behind which Jack hid, liberated from shame and self-consciousness." This moment marks a turning point in the novel because it suggests that social order depends not merely on rules, but on The conch shell becomes the central symbol of democratic order: whoever holds it has the right to speak, and this right is initially respected by all. However, Golding suggests that this arrangement is fundamentally          — it depends not on any natural human tendency toward order, but entirely on the collective decision to respect a shared symbol. The moment individuals prioritise personal desire over collective agreement, the entire system collapses.
