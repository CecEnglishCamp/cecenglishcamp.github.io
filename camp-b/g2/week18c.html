2 Reading · 오늘의 본문
We · Yevgeny Zamyatin · 1924
Carol Kennicott arrived in Gopher Prairie full of dreams. She wanted to change the town, to bring beauty and culture to its quiet, dusty streets. She suggested a new library, a theater group, better schools. But each idea met a wall of polite smiles and silent resistance. The townspeople, whose sense of self depended entirely on the familiar rhythm of their lives, saw her not as a reformer but as a threat. They depended on things staying the same. Her efforts felt like drops of water on stone. Change from within, she learned, was achingly slow, worn down by the quiet, relentless weight of tradition.
B2 · 128 words avg 25.6 w/s
Sinclair Lewis, in 'Main Street', masterfully depicts the profound inertia of social structures through Carol Kennicott's quixotic crusade. Her attempts at reform illuminate the central conflict: the individual's desire for progress against the community's collective investment in stasis. The silent resistance she encounters is not mere stubbornness but a defense mechanism for identities constructed entirely around provincial norms. Lewis suggests that the reformer who remains inside a community wages a war of attrition against an enemy whose very sense of self depends entirely on things staying the same. This internal struggle, therefore, becomes a test of endurance where change is measured not in victories, but in the microscopic erosion of deeply entrenched attitudes over vast stretches of time. What makes Main Street endure as a work of literature is precisely this refusal to offer easy consolation — it demands that readers sit with the discomfort of a world that does not resolve neatly, and find their own position within it.

샘플 에세이
While outsiders can spark change with fresh perspectives, insiders possess crucial cultural understanding. In 'Main Street', Carol, an insider-by-marriage, struggles because her ideas are seen as foreign. Real, sustainable change often requires insiders who can navigate local norms to advocate for reform from a position of trusted belonging. However, as Lewis shows, their path is slow, facing resistance from those whose identities depend on the status quo. Ultimately, a combination of internal will and external catalyst may be most effective.
