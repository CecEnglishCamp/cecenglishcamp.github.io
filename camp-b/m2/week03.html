By the next morning, the Martians had moved closer to London. People who lived in the city began to leave their homes.

The streets that had been quiet the night before were now full of noise. Horses, carts, and hundreds of people who carried their belongings filled every road.

The narrator had left his wife at home, and now he felt terrible about it. He pushed through the crowd and tried to move against the flow.

The Martians that had landed were now building their fighting machines. Each step they took shook the ground. The narrator finally turned and ran. There was no going back now.

London had never seen anything like it. Not in living memory, not in the history books. Every person who could walk was walking, and every person who could run was running.

The narrator pushed against the flow, trying to reach the house where his wife was waiting. He had left her there that morning when the news was still only rumours. He had not believed it would come to this.

A woman stopped him. She was holding a child, and the child was not moving. "Have you seen my husband?" she asked him. He had not. He kept moving.

Behind him, the ground shook. The Martians had arrived at the edge of the city. He could hear the sound now — a low, rhythmic boom, growing louder with each step.

He turned. He ran. He did not look back.
