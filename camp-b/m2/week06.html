Every army that had been sent to fight the Martians had failed. Guns and cannons that were used against the machines had no effect.

Cities that had been proud and strong were now left in ruins. Reports that had come in from the south told of total destruction.

The narrator had lost everything — his home, his wife, his normal life. But he refused to give up.

He moved from place to place, always keeping out of sight. Every day that passed was a small victory. Somewhere in his heart, he still believed the Martians could be defeated.

Defeat is a strange thing to accept when you have no alternative.

The narrator had stopped thinking about winning. Winning was no longer a category that applied. He thought instead about tomorrow, and then the day after that.

He had seen what happened to people who panicked. He had seen what happened to people who gave up. He had decided to be neither.

The countryside was quiet in a way it had never been quiet before. Not peaceful — empty. Every farm he passed had been abandoned. Every road was silent.

But somewhere, in the part of him that was still thinking clearly, he held onto a single idea: that the Martians, for all their power, were still alive. And anything that was alive could die.
